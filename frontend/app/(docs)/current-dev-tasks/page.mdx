## Agentic information-edge simulation (v2)

Already built includes Phases 1-3, including a pure-Python LS-LMSR + ABMM retreat module, three agent classes (credentialed, noise, momentum) plus adversarial tests for H1 (retreat-function robustness). I validated H1 to 1e-9 parity against the original Solidity implementation over 20 determinisitc trades using a tick-based simulator with uniform agent sampling.

The H1 baseline consisted of ~2400 runs to test retreat function robustness. I found that polynomial decay actually performs better than exponential decay and step decay at each liquidity shock magnitude (0%, 25%, 50%, 75%). The H2 baseline consisted of ~1,500 runs to test convergence under mixed populations. I found that the convergence threshold is quite sharp at ~30% credentialed share – below that, convergence is 65-86% but at or above 40%, convergence reaches ≥ 97%.

Core Research Question at the moment: In LS-LMSR event-contract markets with polynomail decay AMM retreat, how do different sources of inforamtion edge (e.g., aggregation depth, cross-market consistency, & tail-event reasoning) contribute to price discovery, and how do they interact in mixed agent populations?

The goal of this model is not to be a strategy comparison between the information edge cases, since these are sources of edge operating at different levels of abstraction. Aggregatipon depth is a single-market inference quality. Tail-event reasoning is prior structure over outcome distributions. Cross-market consistency only exists in a multi-market environment (which I'll subsequently simulate). The simulation I'm proposing studies these three as different processing strategies over the same public information environment (also simulated consistently). I'll frame the simulation more generically (i.e. sector agnostic). The previous dual-layer liquidity model is one instance of LS-LMSR + retreat infra; this simulation applies to any event-contract market with mixed agent populations. Biotech and political prediction markets are both instances of the framework.

The core architectural decisions I had to make were as follows:
    1. Multi-market simulation – cross-market consistency agents require this. Markets share latent factor structure (some correlated clusters, some independent). This simulation factor adds substantial engineering cost but enable the full three-source decomposition.

    2. Continuous-time signaling – signals now arrive as a marked point process (Poisson) rather than per-tick as before. This decouples information arrival from trading decisions, and enables information-flow latency as a distinct metric. However, this requires further event-driven simulation infrastructure in the build.

**Foundational Systems:**

    1. Continuous-time information environment. Essentially a marked point process that generates signals per market with latent factor correlation structure across markets. Each market m has leadings β_m ∈ R^k on a latent factor vector f ∈ R^k plus some idiosyncratic component: p*_m = sigmoid(β_m · f + ε_m). Some markets share strong loadings (correlated cluster); others are dominated by idiosyncratic factors (independent). The environment must integrate separate marked point processes for rare tail events – essentially signals that, when observed, dramatically shift the posterior price view toward extreme outcomes but requires structured reasoning to updated on properly.

    2. Market environment. Essentially the LS-LMSR + polynomial-decay retreat, extended to handle event-driven trading at arbitrary timestamps. The retreat factor updates continuously based on accumulated human volume. Markets evolve in simulated continuous time, parallel-instantiated across the multi-market structure.

    3. Agent population with four classes + noise simulation:

        * Naive credentialed: observes signals on its own market with delay δ (modeling slower human reaction). Follows modal-biased Bayesian inference. Mixed reactive + low-frequency review decision policy. Baseline class which represents what a single human credentialed expert produces.

        * Aggregation-depth – core

        * Cross-market consistency – core

        * Tail-event reasoning – core

        * Noise traders: Poisson-arrival random trades at low rates. Essentially just background flow.

**Outcome Metrics**

    1. Marginal informational contribution by class

    2. Rent-extraction profile

    3. Tail-regime behavior

    4. Information flow latency

## Updated Thoughts on Agentic Trading Applications

Standard prediction market theory assumes information asymmetry comes from differential access to informationn (i.e., Trader A has insider data on a trial, Trader B doesn't. A's trade reveals data and the market converges to truth). All canonical work assumes this structure (Hanson, Chen-Pennock).

Agentic trading breaks the assumption in both directions. In theory, agents reading the same public data shouldn't produce information asymmetry, they should converge to the same prior & make the market a redundant aggregator of price signal. However, the empirical evidence from existing markets shows that even with majority public information training, prices do contain meaningful signal that beats expert forecasts. Therefore, agents reading public data are capable of producing aggregable disagreement.

The primary explanation for why is that information asymmetry in an agentic regime doesn't come from access to facts – it comes from differential inference paths over the same fact-set. Different agents reading the same paper can produce different posteriors if they're applying different reasoning procedures, different priors, or different feature extractors, leading to meaningful signal. The remaining question is who would economically be incentivized to sponsor these agents & how.

**Potential Sponsors:** Three potential classes of sponsors.

    1. Primary-market participants looking to hedge pipeline exposure.

    2. Specialty funds running calibrated agents as alpha-generation strategies.

    3. Protocol-level or platform-incentivized agents.
       
       Most interesting application. Essentially an agentic successor to the ABMM prior. Where the ABMM seeds with a statis off-chain prior and retreats as credentialed-human volume accumulates, a protocol-sponsored agent could continuously update its prior based on new public information, providing a baseline price level that the market can they disagree with or confirm. Informational advantage is breadth (i.e. monitoring all public sources continuously) rather than depth (no single source is secret).

## Multi-agent simulation of Layer 2 LS-LMSR markets

Agents whose trading policies are calibrated against real prediction market behavior, designed to
(a) stress-test the ABMM retreat function;
(b) generate convergence data on simulated event-triggered RWA markets you don't yet have human counterparties for.

Risks & Limitations
1. **Data Problem.** Training agentic policies on Polymarket / Kalshi / PredictIt (i.e. 'platforms') trading history is not obviously transferable to event-triggered biotech RWA markets.

    Potential solution: embrace any mismatch and design around it. Essentially, use existing platform data to calibrate **noise-trader and retail-flow policies**, then synthesize **credentialed-analyst policies** from a structural model.

2. **Fully-autonomous?** What specifically would 'autonomous' entail. Here are a few possibilities:
    
    a. **Rule-Based Agents** with hardcoded policies (momentum traders, mean-reversion traders, conviction-traders, or noise-traders).
    
    b. **Calibrated stochastic agents** whose action distributions are fit to empirical platform trading patterns.
    
    c. **RL-trained or LLM-driven agents** that learn or reason about the market state. Although, convergence behavior would be hard to interpret.

I'll focus efforts on developing (b).

Hypotheses:

1. ABMM retreat exponent is robust to coordinated liquidity-withdrawl attachs below threshold X.

2. With Y% credentialed-conviction agents and Z% noise-trader agents, market price converges to true probability within W trades, regardless of starting prior.

3. LS-LMSR price discovery dominates a constant-product baseline (Uniswap-style) on convergence speed for binary outcomes when one agent class has information advantage.

**Phase 2: Agent Classes & Integration**
    1. CredentialedTrader – informed agent. This agent class makes the market converge in Hypothesis 2 (mixed agent composition, convergence within W trades, regardless of init. prior).
        Design notes: Each cred_trader gets a private signal s_i ~ Normal(true_prob, σ_i²). Smaller σ_i would imply more accurate signal. The trader would trade toward s_i, not toward true_prob directly. This matters because in real markets, even credentialed analysts don't know the true probability – rather based on noisy estimates. A market populated entirely by credentialed traders should converge to the average of their signals, which itself converges to true_prob as the pop. grows. Additionally, we have optionality for how the agent should size its trade. (a) Edge-proportional sizing – trade size proportional to |signal – market_price|. Bigger disagreement = bigger trade. Or, (b) Kelly-like sizing – trade size proportional to fractional edge (signal – price) / price for YES, or (price – signal) / (1 – price) for NO. Closer to how informed traders behave in true markets, but much more volatile. Since Kelly-like sizing produces more random-walk trades near the boundaries (price near 0 or 1), I'll stick to edge-proportional sizing.

    2. MomentumTrader – trend follower. This agent class delays convergence in some H2 mixes and amplifies attacks in H1 (liquidity withdrawl attacks below threshold X).
        Design notes: We'll pay attention to the lookback window. Short lookback (3-5 trades) creates noisy momentum that can flip directions rapidly. Longer lookback (50+ trades) creates slow, persistent momentum that strongly amplifies drift. For our second hypothesis, we'll choose a mid-level lookback (10-20 trades), but for our first hypothesis focused on withdrawl liquidity-shocks, I'll use longer lookbacks since it'd cause momentum traders more likely to amplify the adversarial attack – i.e. better stress test.

    3. AdversarialTrader – coordinated-attack agent for H1. Implements the liquidity-withdrawl-then-push attack we're testing the exponential reatreat function against.
        Design notes: simpler design. First phase of any Adversarial Trader accumulates a position quietly, and then at trade T, drumps aggressively in one direction to push price. The question is whether the ABMM retreat function pushes back. The interesting parameter is the attack magnitude as a fraction of total liquidity at attack time.

    4. NoiseTrader (already built) – random directly, normally-distributed size.

    5. Unit tests for each class.
    
    6. Property tests for the credentialed trader.
    
    7. Integration test that runs all four together and confirms the runner doesn't break.

    By the end of this block, we should have a populated market with all four agent classes running for 1,000 trades, producing a trajectory plot, with the credentialed-only test showing convergence to true probability.
